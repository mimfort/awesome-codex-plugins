# Book Reader Conversations Rules

Use these rules when planning or synthesizing pre-draft conversations for useful nonfiction.

## Core Rules

### 1. Start With A Learning Goal

Choose one main uncertainty per conversation round.

- Validate reader urgency before optimizing TOC polish.
- Validate TOC order before drafting chapters in full.
- Validate examples and exercises before preparing beta readers.

### 2. Ask About Life, Not The Idea

Avoid questions that invite compliments or speculation.

- Ask what happened, what they tried, what failed, and what they searched for.
- Ask where they got stuck and what they gave up on.
- Ask what advice, books, tools, or communities helped or wasted time.

### 3. Separate Listening From Teaching

Use listening when the reader, problem, or promise is uncertain. Use teaching when the author has a promising slice of TOC to test.

- In listening mode, do not pitch unless needed for context.
- In teaching mode, help the reader get value rather than describing what the book will contain.
- In both modes, record confusion, skepticism, missing prerequisites, and exact phrases.

### 4. Prefer Warm, Relevant Readers

Reader quality matters more than volume.

- Begin with people close to the target reader profile.
- Prioritize people currently facing or recently facing the problem.
- Treat inability to find any relevant reader as a serious book-risk signal.

### 5. Turn Notes Into Decisions

Do not stop at a transcript summary.

- Convert repeated language into promise and TOC wording.
- Convert confusion into missing sections, examples, or reordered material.
- Convert weak urgency into narrower reader or problem selection.
- Convert successful teaching into draftable chapter structure.

### 6. Preserve Reader Trust

Conversations should be useful and respectful.

- Be explicit that the goal is learning.
- Do not pressure readers into beta reading, testimonials, or promotion.
- Offer useful help when running a teaching conversation.

## Guidelines

- Run a small batch first: 3-5 conversations can expose major scope issues.
- Capture both what readers say and what they avoid.
- Note when readers become animated, ask follow-up questions, or volunteer examples.
- Save strong reader phrases in a quote bank for later copy and TOC work.
- Mark promising beta-reader leads with why they are a fit.

## Exceptions

- **Expert books**: Experts may want to evaluate the idea earlier, but still ask for concrete cases and examples.
- **Memoir or narrative nonfiction**: Conversations may focus on resonance, relevance, and reader transformation rather than procedural outcomes.
- **Internal company books**: The target reader may be mandatory, but conversations still reveal assumptions and missing context.

## Quick Reference

| Rule | Summary |
|------|---------|
| Learning goal | Know what uncertainty the conversation tests. |
| Life first | Ask about real experience, not opinions. |
| Mode choice | Listen for scope; teach for effectiveness. |
| Relevant readers | Prefer a few strong matches over many weak ones. |
| Decisions | Translate notes into book changes. |
| Trust | Make participation useful and low-pressure. |
