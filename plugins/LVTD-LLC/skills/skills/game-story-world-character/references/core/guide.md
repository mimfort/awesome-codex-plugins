# Game Story World Character Guide

## Story Roles In Games

Story can:

- create motivation for action
- make goals meaningful
- help players predict world logic
- create emotional stakes
- provide mystery and anticipation
- explain constraints
- make repeated actions feel contextual
- support identity, roleplay, or expression

If the story does not change what the player wants, understands, or feels, keep it light.

## Story-Gameplay Fit

For every beat, define:

- Player action that causes, reveals, or responds to the beat.
- System state required for the beat.
- Gameplay consequence after the beat.
- What happens if the player fails, delays, skips, or repeats it.

Avoid narrative that demands urgency while mechanics reward slow optimization unless that tension is intentional.

## Indirect Control Tools

- visible goals
- lighting, contrast, motion, and sound
- spatial composition
- enemy or NPC attention
- rewards and collectibles
- camera framing
- level chokepoints and affordances
- UI priority and quest markers
- character dialogue and social pressure

## Character Spec

Useful character design includes:

- Gameplay role: guide, obstacle, rival, vendor, companion, target, mystery, comic relief, or mirror.
- Desire: what the character wants in world terms.
- Player relationship: why the player cares or responds.
- Behavioral rules: what the character does in common states.
- Dialogue constraints: vocabulary, tone, length, and what they never say.
- State hooks: flags, quests, affinity, inventory, location, or world events.

## World Rules

Worldbuilding should constrain implementation:

- What is common?
- What is rare?
- What is impossible?
- What does the world reward or punish?
- What technology, magic, economy, ecology, or social rule affects play?
- What visual and audio motifs signal meaning?
