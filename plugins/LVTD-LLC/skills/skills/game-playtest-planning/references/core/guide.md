# Game Playtest Planning Guide

## Playtest Question Types

- Comprehension: do players understand goals, controls, rules, feedback, and consequences?
- Feel: does the core action produce the intended sensation?
- Balance: where do players succeed, fail, exploit, or disengage?
- Motivation: what makes players continue or stop?
- Usability: can players navigate menus, settings, tutorials, and UI?
- Content: which levels, encounters, puzzles, or beats land?
- Retention: does the game create a reason to return?

## Method Selection

- Silent observation: best for seeing natural behavior.
- Think-aloud: useful for discovering interpretation, but it can change play behavior.
- Task-based usability test: best for onboarding, UI, and specific flows.
- Blind first-session test: best for real first impressions.
- Comparative test: useful when choosing between two designs.
- Telemetry review: useful for scale, but weak without qualitative interpretation.
- Interview or survey: useful after play, but less reliable than observed behavior for moment-to-moment problems.

## Moderator Rules

- Do not teach unless the test explicitly allows help.
- Do not defend the design.
- Ask what the player expected, noticed, and tried.
- Separate observed behavior from player opinion.
- Record exact moments where confusion, delight, frustration, or mastery appeared.
- Let silence work; players often reveal more after thinking.

## Severity Rubric

- Critical: blocks progress, breaks the prototype question, or causes abandonment.
- Major: causes repeated confusion, unfair failure, or wrong mental model.
- Moderate: causes friction but players recover.
- Minor: polish issue or isolated annoyance.
