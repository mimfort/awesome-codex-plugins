# Player Experience Modeling Guide

## Player Model

Useful player models describe behavior:

- Why the player starts.
- What they already understand.
- What they want to feel competent at.
- What they will tolerate learning.
- What they are likely to misunderstand.
- What session length and device context they bring.
- What makes them return.

Avoid shallow demographic-only personas. Age or genre familiarity can matter, but design decisions need motives, skills, context, and friction.

## Experience Promise

Write the promise as:

> For players who want [motive], this game lets them [core activity] so they can feel [experience].

Then test every major feature against that promise.

## Motivation Lenses

- Autonomy: the player feels ownership over choices, expression, path, or style.
- Competence: the player sees improvement and understands why outcomes happen.
- Relatedness: the player feels connected to characters, friends, rivals, community, or shared context.
- Novelty: the player encounters fresh situations before patterns become stale.
- Mastery: the player can improve through practice, planning, or understanding.
- Fantasy: the player inhabits a role, world, or capability they value.
- Discovery: the player uncovers systems, spaces, secrets, or story.

## Flow And Friction

For each segment, compare challenge to player ability:

- Too easy: boredom, autopilot, or grind.
- Too hard: anxiety, confusion, or resignation.
- Unclear: players cannot tell whether the problem is skill, rules, controls, or luck.
- Good tension: players understand the goal and believe effort can change the outcome.

## Interest Curve Beats

Map:

- Hook: first clear promise.
- Ramp: safe learning and rising complexity.
- Variation: new pattern before repetition dulls attention.
- Peak: high-intensity moment supported by previous learning.
- Recovery: lower intensity that gives space to process and choose.
- Return hook: unresolved desire that invites another session.
